## Design Context

### Users

**Teachers (primary, desktop-first)**
The core user. Course creation is their primary — and non-trivial — activity. They spend long sessions building curricula, managing students, and running live lessons. The interface must feel like a professional tool: capable, spacious, and respectful of their expertise. They should feel accomplished when they finish a session.

**Students (mobile/tablet/laptop)**
Secondary users who consume content across a wide range of devices, often on phones and iPads. All pages — including the course builder — must be responsive. The experience is intentionally richer on desktop: Neptino is to education what a professional cinema screen is to film. A phone is the living room TV: fully supported, but a step down. Responsive layouts are required everywhere; the course builder setup view already sets the pattern for how builder views adapt, and create/preview/launch views will follow the same approach. Never let mobile constraints drive decisions that compromise the desktop experience.

**Admins (desktop, occasional mobile for trivial tasks)**
Power users of the management layer. Desktop-first. Efficiency and clarity matter more than expressiveness.

### Brand Personality

**Three words:** Trustworthy. Playful (not childish). Expansive.

**Voice:** Confident without being corporate. Warm without being casual. The tone of a brilliant professor who also happens to be good company.

**Emotional goal:** When a teacher finishes building a course or ends a session, they should feel **accomplished** — like they've built something real and substantial.

**Metaphor:** Neptino is the cinema screen. The full canvas, live sessions, multi-layered course builder — these are the big screen experience. Phones are the TV at home. Never sacrifice the cinematic for the portable.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Visual tone:** Professional and minimal, but with genuine warmth and a sense of scale. Not sterile. Not corporate. Not vibe-coded.

**Anti-references:** Anything that looks AI-generated / vibe-coded (gradient soup, generic SaaS purple, confetti dashboards). Anything that looks like enterprise software (grey tables, dense form grids, SharePoint energy). Overly playful or childish edtech (Duolingo-style gamification, cartoon mascots).

**References (inferred):** Linear, Figma, Notion at their most considered — tools that feel like they were designed by people who care. The Atlas/encyclopedia system is an expression of the same design language, not a separate product — it uses contextual tokens from the same system, just applied with different weight and intention.

**Theme:** Light mode primary. Dark mode defined and supported. One unified product: The Atlas/encyclopedia surfaces use the shared Neptino design system — desaturated accent colors and Open Sans — never a separate visual register.

### Color System

**Philosophy:** Black and white dominate. Color is used purposefully — never decoratively. All accent colors are desaturated versions of the spectrum, never vivid or saturated.

### Typography System

| Font | Variable | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| Open Sans | `--font-open-sans` | Primary display and body font across the product — high legibility, neutral authority |
| Geist Sans | `--font-geist-sans` | UI chrome — forms, labels, navigation |
| Geist Mono | `--font-geist-mono` | Code, technical content |

Cormorant Garamond and Instrument Sans have been removed. Open Sans at `font-weight: 300` with light tracking replaces the old `.atlas-serif` display role — differentiation through weight, not typeface.

### Design Principles

1. **The canvas earns its space.** The core product is a large-format canvas experience. All pages are responsive — including the course builder — but the desktop is the cinematic experience. Every layout decision should reinforce the feeling of working in a spacious, capable environment. Responsive layouts adapt gracefully; they never dictate desktop design choices.

2. **Trustworthy before delightful.** Clarity, consistency, and reliability come first. Delight is earned through precision — not decoration. A well-placed hover state is more valuable than a bouncing illustration.

3. **One unified product.** The Atlas/encyclopedia surfaces are part of the same design system — desaturated accent colors and Open Sans used contextually, not as a separate layer. Everything in Neptino should feel like it belongs to the same family.

4. **Sparse > busy.** Whitespace is not wasted space — it's what gives the canvas its authority. Resist the urge to fill voids. Let content breathe.

5. **No emojis. No vibe-coding.** Every visual decision should be deliberate. SVG icons only (Lucide + custom set in `public/icons-coursebuilder/`). No gradient overload. No rounded-everything-to-3xl. Rounded corners are `0.5rem` base — present but restrained.

### Notes on Existing Design State

The design system exists but is currently decentralized:
- Color tokens are well-defined in `src/app/globals.css`
- Typography is loaded in `src/app/layout.tsx` but usage conventions are informal
- UI primitives are sparse (`src/components/ui/` has only 5 files)
- The Atlas system has the richest, most intentional visual language in the codebase
- The marketing homepage (`src/app/page.tsx`) and teacher dashboard are the most developed UI surfaces

When skills improve existing surfaces, they should consolidate toward these tokens and principles rather than introduce new patterns.
